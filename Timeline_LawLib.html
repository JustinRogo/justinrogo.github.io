1921 - 1931

Downtown Hartford

The law school operated out of rented space at several downtown locations, including 94 Allyn Street and the Hartford Trust Building. During this period, there was no dedicated law library, and students relied on the Hartford County Bar Library.

1940 - 1964

39 Woodland Street

The move to 39 Woodland Street significantly increased available space. A later annex added dedicated stack areas and a reading room.

From the Archives

Caroline Lillard, co-founder, managed the library 1940-1942.
Obituary: George Lillard passed away October 25, 1940.
Margaret Taylor Lane appointed librarian. Ms. Taylor Lane's career would mostly take place in Louisiana but for two years, she oversaw the law library. She was a graduate of both the Louisiana State Law School and Columbia University School of Library Science. Board Minutes from January 16, 1946, show the Board accepting her resignation in that year. She went on work at the Louisiana State as Law Librarian and lecturer on Legal Bibliography and later as recorder of documents for the state of Louisiana.
Leon M. Liddell named Law Librarian. Liddell earned his legal degree at the University of Texas and was admitted to the Texas Bar. He worked both in private practice and for Insurance companies in the claims divisions before coming to the Law Library, which at that time was now the University of Connecticut School of Law. After a short stint in Connecticut, Liddell moved on to the University of Minnesota for the law librarian position there.
Karl Punzak put constant pressure on the Administration for increases to the library budget and the collection grew from 15,000 to over 22,000 volumes during this tenure. He also directed planning for a new wing at the 39 Woodland Street building. The entire second floor of this addition housed the main reading room of the Library.
Eileen M. Murphytook over as law librarian in 1956. Ms. Murphy had an MLS (Master of Library Science) from Pratt Institute Library School in New York City and a LL.B. from St. John's University. She moved on to an illustrious career after leaving UConn. She was hired as the first female attorney at General Motors Corporation in 1959 and played an important role in establishing the American Association of Law Librarians' (AALL) headquarters in Chicago.
Shirley Raissi Bysiewicz oversaw the move of the library to 1800 Asylum and staffing of a new space. She was a strong advocate for the library and staff. Bysiewicz returned to her alma mater in 1956 to teach and in 1972 became the first female tenured professor at the University of Connecticut School of Law. She served as a law librarian and later library director. Bysiewicz is widely credited and commended for the growth and quality of the library's collections during her time as Director.

Jessica de Perio Wittman, Director 2018-present. de Perio Wittman has been director during a move from less print to more digital to online learning. During her time, law librarians have increased courses taught in legal research and writing, and added ones on access to justice and diversity in the law among others. Librarians also provide in class sessions on using legal databases, and provide one on one consulting to students for papers and projects. They also provide indepth research services for faculty.
